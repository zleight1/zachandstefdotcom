BEDEKEN

Veiling the bride
Stefanie is seated on a bridal chair, and is greeted by friends and family members. Feel free to come see Stefanie and wish her a Mazal Tov! Once the ketubah is signed, the father’s of the bride and groom escort the groom to the bride, where everyone is welcomed to sing and dance. There, Zach will place the veil over Stefanie’s face. The roots of this custom date back at least 600 years, and is based on Talmudic sources. Perhaps the ceremony signifies modesty, or Zach’s responsibility—defined in the Ketubah-for clothing Stefanie. Or Zach may be symbolically confirming the identity of the bride. Zach and Stefanie will be blessed by their fathers, as they prepare for their life together. At this point, Zach is again escorted by everyone singing and dancing, to prepare for the ceremony.

TISCH

Breaking the plate
In a room down the hall, Zach meets with his friends and family who may sing and share the celebration. He may attempt to give a brief lecture on some issue in Jewish law; if so, custom dictates that he be interrupted by his friends and family singing. At the Tisch, the Tena’im- literally translated as “The Engagement”, is read by a close friend or rabbi. In Jewish law, the engagement is more than just the intention to marry; it carries considerable legal and social significance. Then, the mothers of the bride and groom break a china plate, signifying the completion of the engagement agreement. Feel free to greet Zach and wish him a Mazal Tov!

CHUPPAH

The marriage ceremony
The ceremony itself is a combination of symbolism, traditions and religiously binding acts. The central physical symbol is the Chuppah, the marriage canopy. It represents the home that they will create together, and the Divine Presence under which they will be married.
The seating at the Chuppah is separate, men are welcome to sit on one side, and women on the other side. Just as men and women sit separately during religious services.

Zach’s Aufruf

The shabbat before the wedding it is customary for the groom to lead the services at the synagogue. This includes leading the prayers, and being called up to the Torah for an Aliyah as a “Chatan”, a groom. It is customary to throw candy at Zach after he concludes the blessings, as a way to “shower him with sweetness and blessings” before his wedding.
